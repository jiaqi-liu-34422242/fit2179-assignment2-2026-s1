Section 02

Who Travels How?

Travel behaviour varies by trip purpose, age group, and income. Data from the Victorian Integrated Survey of Travel and Activity (VISTA) reveals which groups depend most on public transport.

Trip Purpose and Mode Choice

Heat map: rows encode trip purposes, columns encode mode groups, and colour luminance encodes trip share percentage.

Source: VISTA, Department of Transport and Planning, 2024

Mode Share by Age Group

100% stacked bar chart: each age group is normalised to 100%, and segment length shows each mode's share.

Source: VISTA, 2024

Public Transport Share by Income Band

Dot plot: each point represents one ordered income band. Horizontal position on a common scale encodes public transport trip share.

Source: VISTA, 2024

The heat map shows how mode choice changes across trip purposes. The 100% stacked bars then compare travel composition across age groups, while the dot plot treats income as an ordered category rather than sorting it by value. Together, these charts show transport dependency without mixing up categorical hue and quantitative colour luminance.

Section 03

Melbourne Stop Coverage

Because most Victorians live in metropolitan Melbourne, this section focuses on stop coverage within Greater Melbourne LGAs. Stop density is used as a network coverage measure: it shows where stops are spatially concentrated, but it does not directly measure service frequency or demand.

Public Transport Stop Density across Greater Melbourne

Choropleth map: each LGA polygon is shaded by stops per square kilometre. Colour luminance encodes the quantitative stop-density value.

Source: Transport Victoria Open Data; ABS LGA boundaries 2021

Highest and Lowest Stop Density LGAs in Melbourne

Dot plot: each point represents one LGA. Horizontal position on a common scale encodes stops per square kilometre.

Source: Derived from Transport Victoria Open Data and ABS LGA boundaries 2021

Inner LGAs such as Yarra, Melbourne, Stonnington and Port Phillip show the densest stop coverage because many tram, train and bus stops are packed into small land areas. Outer growth LGAs such as Cardinia, Yarra Ranges and Nillumbik have lower stop density because stops are spread across much larger areas.

Stop density is a coverage indicator, not a service-quality indicator. A high-density LGA may still have uneven frequency or accessibility, while a low-density regional-edge LGA may cover a much larger land area.

Section 04

Patronage over Time

Patronage data shows how public transport demand changed after major disruptions and how weekday travel still differs from weekend travel. This section uses time as the main structure, so the chart choices follow the course-note distinction between ordered trends and categorical comparison.

Monthly Public Transport Patronage by Mode

Line chart: month is an ordered temporal attribute, vertical position encodes monthly patronage, and hue separates transport modes.

Source: Department of Transport and Planning Victoria, monthly public transport patronage by mode

The line chart supports trend reading: the same modes are tracked across monthly time points, making drops and recovery patterns visible. Metropolitan train, tram, and bus remain the largest modes, while regional train is shown as a smaller but distinct series.

The grouped bar chart changes the task from trend analysis to categorical comparison. It compares average weekday and weekend patronage in 2025, using bar length from a shared zero baseline.

Weekday vs Weekend Patronage

Grouped bar chart: each mode is a category, bar length encodes average daily patronage, and hue separates normal weekdays from weekends.

Source: Department of Transport and Planning Victoria, 2025 average daily patronage

Section 05

Tourism and Regional Rail

To extend the transport story beyond commuting, this section examines whether Victoria's tourism regions are well connected to the regional rail network and how overnight tourism demand has changed over time.

Tourism Regions and Their Nearest Regional Train Stations

Network diagram: tourism regions and regional train stations are represented as nodes, while links show the nearest-station connection for each region.

Source: Transport Victoria Open Data and tourism region centres derived for visual analysis.

Rail Access Difficulty by Tourism Region

Grid map: each tourism region is shown as one equal-sized cell. Colour luminance encodes distance to the nearest regional train station.

Source: Derived from tourism region centres and regional train stops.

The network diagram reveals the nearest-station dependency structure, while the grid map removes differences in region size so access difficulty can be compared more fairly across all tourism regions.

Regions with long distances to the nearest train station may still attract visitors, but they place a heavier burden on car travel or last-mile transport.

Rail Access Redundancy around Tourism Regions

Bubble plot: horizontal position shows distance to the nearest regional train station, vertical position shows how many regional train stations sit within 50 km, and bubble size shows how many sit within 100 km.

Source: Derived from tourism region centres and regional train stops.

Overnight Tourism Demand Composition over Time

Stacked area chart: visitor trips are grouped into intrastate overnight, interstate overnight, and international overnight segments from 2020 to 2025.

Source: Victorian Tourism Statistics, year ending December 2025.

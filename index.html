Section 01

Where Is the Network?

Victoria's public transport network spans train, tram, bus, and regional coach services — but stops are overwhelmingly concentrated in metropolitan Melbourne.
The maps use LGA (Local Government Area) boundaries, which represent local council areas.

Victoria - Full State

interactive point map using position and hue. It shows that Victoria has a statewide public transport footprint, but the visible mass of stops is concentrated around metropolitan Melbourne.

Melbourne - Zoom In

zoomed point map using position and colour by mode. It reveals how Melbourne's inner network is much denser, with tram and train stops clustered around the CBD and bus stops filling the wider suburban fabric.

Source: Transport Victoria Open Data - GTFS Schedule; ABS ASGS LGA boundaries 2021.

Transport Mode Composition across Victoria

waffle chart for part-to-whole comparison, where each cell represents about 1% of stops. It shows the network is numerically dominated by bus stops, while train, tram, and regional stops form much smaller shares.

Source: Transport Victoria Open Data, 2024

This section turns the free-fare question into an access question: lower prices matter only when people can reach the network.

Access baseline The state map shows where public transport physically reaches before any fare policy is considered.
Metro pull The stop pattern is heavily concentrated around Melbourne, so benefits are not spatially even.
Bus reach Because buses make up most mapped stops, a free-fare future depends strongly on local and suburban bus coverage.
Equity risk Areas with fewer nearby stops may still face an access barrier even when the fare barrier is removed.
The key issue is not price alone. Free fares reduce the cost barrier, but unequal stop coverage still shapes who can benefit most from the policy.

Section 02

Who Travels How?

Travel behaviour varies by trip purpose, age group, and income. Data from the Victorian Integrated Survey of Travel and Activity (VISTA) reveals which groups depend most on public transport.

9trip purposes compared by mode group
19age bands normalised to 100%

Trip Purpose and Mode Choice

heat map with trip purpose as rows, mode groups as columns, and colour luminance for share. It shows public transport is most important for education and work-related trips, while car travel dominates pick-up, shopping, and accompanying trips.

Source: VISTA, Department of Transport and Planning, 2024

Mode Share by Age Group

100% stacked bar chart normalising each age group to the same width. It shows teenagers and young adults have the highest public transport shares, while older age groups rely more heavily on cars.

Source: VISTA, 2024

Public Transport Share by Income Band

ordered lollipop chart using a shared horizontal scale for public transport share. It shows the highest PT reliance in the lowest positive income band and negative-income group, making fare relief most relevant to financially constrained travellers.

Source: VISTA, 2024

Together, these three charts reveal that public transport dependence is not uniform. Younger travellers and lower-income households rely on public transport more heavily, while commuting and education trips dominate mode choice far more than shopping or social travel.

A free-fare policy would disproportionately benefit the groups already most dependent on public transport: younger residents and lower-income households.

Section 03

Melbourne Stop Coverage

Because most Victorians live in metropolitan Melbourne, this section focuses on stop coverage within Greater Melbourne LGAs. Stop density is used as a network coverage measure: it shows where stops are spatially concentrated, but it does not directly measure service frequency or demand.

LGAboundaries support local comparison
km²normalisation reduces area-size bias

Public Transport Stop Density across Greater Melbourne

interactive choropleth map encoding stops per km2 by colour across LGAs. It shows inner LGAs such as Yarra, Melbourne, Stonnington, and Port Phillip have the densest coverage, while outer growth-area LGAs are more sparsely served.

Source: Transport Victoria Open Data; ABS LGA boundaries 2021

Distribution of Stop Density across Melbourne LGAs

histogram with 5-stop-per-km2 bins and a median reference line. It shows coverage is uneven: many LGAs sit below 10 stops per km2, while a smaller group of inner LGAs creates the high-density tail.

Source: Derived from Transport Victoria Open Data and ABS LGA boundaries 2021

The histogram complements the choropleth by showing the overall distribution, rather than repeating an ordered list of high and low LGAs. It makes clear that dense inner coverage is the exception, not the Melbourne-wide norm.

Inner LGAs such as Yarra, Melbourne, Stonnington and Port Phillip show the densest stop coverage because many tram, train and bus stops are packed into small land areas.

Stop density is a coverage indicator, not a service-quality indicator. A high-density LGA may still have uneven frequency or accessibility, while a low-density regional-edge LGA may cover a much larger land area.

Patronage over Time

Patronage data shows how public transport demand changed after major disruptions and how weekday travel still differs from weekend travel. This section uses time as the main structure, so the chart choices follow the course-note distinction between ordered trends and categorical comparison.

2day types compared in the slope graph

Monthly Public Transport Patronage by Mode

multi-series line chart using temporal position for month and hue for mode. It shows the sharp disruption in 2020, gradual recovery afterwards, and the continuing dominance of metropolitan train, tram, and bus patronage.

Source: Department of Transport and Planning Victoria, monthly public transport patronage by mode

Weekday vs Weekend Patronage

slope graph linking weekday and weekend patronage for each mode on one scale. It shows every mode drops on weekends, with metropolitan train and bus showing especially large weekday-to-weekend gaps.

Source: Department of Transport and Planning Victoria, 2025 average daily patronage

The line chart supports trend reading: the same modes are tracked across monthly time points, making drops and recovery patterns visible. Metropolitan train, tram, and bus remain the largest modes, while regional train is shown as a smaller but distinct series.

The slope graph narrows the time comparison to two ordered states. It makes the weekday-to-weekend change visible for each mode without introducing another bar-family chart.

Tourism and Regional Rail

Rail Access Difficulty by Tourism Region

ranked bar chart using length and ordered position for nearest-station distance. It shows Phillip Island, the Grampians, and Great Ocean Road have the largest rail access gaps, while Melbourne, Geelong, Goldfields, Murray, and Gippsland sit close to a station.

Regions with long distances to the nearest train station may still attract visitors, but they place a heavier burden on car travel or last-mile transport.

bubble plot using x-position for nearest-station distance, y-position for stations within 50 km, and size for stations within 100 km. It shows that some regions may be near one station but have little backup access, whereas Melbourne has both proximity and many alternatives.

Source: Derived from tourism region centres and regional train stops.
